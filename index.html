The Origins of the Cold War

How did the spread of communism in Europe after 1945 lead to Cold War tensions?

Source 1A
0/50 marks · Q1/20
Skills Guide — Extraction

How to extract evidence from a source

Extraction questions ask you to find specific information that is directly stated in the source. The answer is IN the text — you don't need to interpret or add your own knowledge.

1
Read the question carefully. Underline the KEY WORDS. What exactly are you being asked to find? A person? A reason? A place? How many?
2
Scan the source for those key words or their synonyms. The answer is usually within one or two sentences of where the key words appear.
3
Check your answer is IN the source. If you can't point to the exact sentence where your answer comes from, you're probably interpreting rather than extracting.
4
Watch for "how many": If the question says "identify TWO reasons," you need exactly two. Don't give one, don't give three.
"According to the source, who replaced Byrnes?" → Scan for "Byrnes" in the source → Find "with Byrnes out and George Marshall in" → Answer: George Marshall.
Common mistakes: Using your own knowledge instead of the source. Giving information that is true but not stated in THIS specific source. Providing more or fewer points than asked for.
Skills Guide — Concept Definition

How to define a historical concept "in your own words"

"In your own words" means you cannot just repeat what the source says. You must show that you understand what the concept means in its historical context.

1
Identify what KIND of thing it is. Is it a policy? An event? An ideology? A metaphor? Start your definition by placing it in a category.
2
Explain what it means — not just what it is. Don't just name it; explain what it involved, who it affected, and why it mattered.
3
Connect it to the historical context. "In the context of" means linking your definition to the specific period, event, or topic in the question.
"Define Stalinisation in the context of the Cold War" → Category: a process/policy → What it involved: imposing Stalin's model of governance → Specifics: banning opposition, purging dissidents, state control of economy → Context: applied across Eastern Europe after 1948 to consolidate Soviet control.
Common mistakes: Simply repeating the source's words (not "your own words"). Giving a vague or generic definition without historical context. Confusing similar concepts (e.g. Iron Curtain vs Berlin Wall).
Skills Guide — Interpretation

How to interpret evidence from a source

Interpretation questions ask you to go beyond what the source says to explain what it means or implies. You're reading between the lines.

1
Start with what the source SAYS (the evidence), then explain what it MEANS (the interpretation). Use phrases like "This suggests that…", "This implies…", "The significance of this is…"
2
Ask WHY, not just WHAT. If the source describes something happening, ask yourself: why did it happen? Why does it matter? What are the consequences?
3
Consider the author's PURPOSE. Why did they say it this way? What effect were they trying to create? What words did they choose and why?
4
For "comment on" questions: Give your view supported by evidence. "Comment on" = interpret + explain significance. Always link back to the bigger picture.
"Comment on why Acheson called the meeting 'Armageddon'" → WHAT: he used an extreme, apocalyptic term → WHY: because he believed failure to act would lead to communist domination of three continents → SIGNIFICANCE: this reveals the Cold War mentality where every decision was seen as existential.
Common mistakes: Just repeating what the source says without explaining what it means. Describing events without analysing their significance. Giving your personal opinion without supporting it from the source.
Skills Guide — Source + Own Knowledge

How to use a source AND your own knowledge together

These questions require two things: evidence from the source AND additional information you've studied. You need both — one without the other won't earn full marks.

1
Identify what the SOURCE tells you. What evidence or clues does it provide? Start your answer with what you can see or read in the source.
2
Add what you KNOW from your studies. What additional facts, context, or background information helps explain what the source shows? This is your own knowledge.
3
LINK them together. Don't just list source evidence and own knowledge separately. Show how your own knowledge EXPLAINS what the source shows, or how the source CONFIRMS what you know.
"Using the source and your own knowledge, explain why communism didn't expand into Greece and Turkey" → SOURCE: the cartoon shows Greece and Turkey outside the Soviet bloc → OWN KNOWLEDGE: the Truman Doctrine (1947) provided US military and economic aid specifically to these countries → LINK: the source shows the outcome; my knowledge explains the cause.
Common mistakes: Using only the source without adding own knowledge. Using only own knowledge without referencing the source. Listing both without connecting them. Using own knowledge that doesn't relate to the question.
Skills Guide — Source Comparison

How to compare two sources

Comparison questions ask you to find connections between sources. How does one source support, contradict, or add to another? You must discuss BOTH sources — not each one separately.

1
Identify the THEME that connects them. What topic or idea do both sources address? Start by finding what they have in common.
2
Find specific EVIDENCE in each source that relates to this shared theme. You need concrete details from both, not vague summaries.
3
Explain the CONNECTION. Use linking phrases: "Source A states… and this is supported/confirmed/extended by Source B which shows…" Don't describe each source separately.
4
For 4-mark questions: give TWO comparison points. Each point should identify a theme, cite evidence from both sources, and explain the connection. That's 2×2 = 4 marks.
"How does Source 1A support Source 1C?" → THEME: growing communist threat → SOURCE 1A: Churchill identifies countries already under Soviet control (1946) → SOURCE 1C: Acheson warns the threat has spread to Greece and Turkey (1947) → CONNECTION: Source 1C shows the very pattern Source 1A warned about was continuing to escalate.
Common mistakes: Describing each source separately without making connections. Saying sources "are similar" without explaining HOW. Comparing the authors instead of the content. Saying sources can't be compared because they discuss different events.
Skills Guide — Paragraph Writing

How to write an 8-mark paragraph

The paragraph question tests your ability to synthesise — pulling together source evidence and your own knowledge into a coherent, analytical argument. It's assessed holistically (as a whole), not point by point.

1
TOPIC SENTENCE: Address the question directly. State your argument in one sentence. This tells the examiner you understand what's being asked. Don't start with background — start with your answer.
2
SOURCE EVIDENCE: Use specific evidence from at least TWO sources. Reference them by number (Source 1A, Source 1C). Don't just name the source — use its content as proof for your argument.
3
OWN KNOWLEDGE: Add at least ONE piece of information that isn't in the sources. This proves you've studied the topic, not just read the addendum. It must support your argument, not just be a random fact.
4
CONCLUSION: Tie the paragraph together. Return to the question and show how your evidence answers it. The best conclusions name the mechanism — HOW and WHY things happened, not just WHAT happened.
Level 3 (6–8 marks): "The spread of communism after 1945 created escalating tensions as Soviet expansion provoked Western containment. Churchill warned that an Iron Curtain had divided Europe (Source 1A), and Acheson argued the threat extended to three continents (Source 1C). The USA responded with the Truman Doctrine and Marshall Plan to contain communism. Thus, a self-reinforcing cycle of expansion and containment divided Europe into hostile blocs."
Level 1 (0–2) mistakes: Writing a list of facts instead of an argument. No topic sentence. No source references. No own knowledge. No conclusion. Just describing WHAT happened without explaining WHY or HOW.

Length: About 8 lines / 80 words. Quality over quantity — a focused, well-structured short paragraph beats a long, rambling one.

Skills Guide — Reliability

How to evaluate the reliability of a source

Reliability questions ask: can a historian trust this source? The answer is almost never simply "yes" or "no." You need to weigh strengths against limitations.

1
WHO created it? What was their position? Did they have first-hand knowledge or insider access? Were they an eyewitness? An expert? A politician with an agenda?
2
WHEN was it created? Was it during the events (contemporary = more immediate, but possibly less reflective) or after (retrospective = more perspective, but memory may fade)?
3
WHY was it created? What was the purpose? To inform? To persuade? To justify? To rally support? Purpose shapes content — a political speech has different reliability than a private diary.
4
WHAT perspective does it represent? Does it show only one side? Would the other side tell a different story? A Western source on Soviet expansion will frame things differently than a Soviet source.
"This source is reliable because Churchill had first-hand knowledge of Soviet intentions from wartime conferences (WHO) and the speech was delivered during the period of emerging Cold War tensions (WHEN). However, its purpose was to rally American support against the USSR (WHY), and it represents only a Western perspective (PERSPECTIVE)."
Common mistakes: Don't say "reliable because it's a primary source" — primary sources can be biased. Don't say "unreliable because it's biased" — all sources have a perspective; bias doesn't automatically mean unreliable. Don't confuse "the author's opinion" with "unreliable" — an informed opinion from an insider IS valuable evidence, even if it's one-sided.

The key move: Show the examiner you can hold TWO ideas at once — the source IS valuable for certain reasons AND has limitations for other reasons. That's what earns full marks.
